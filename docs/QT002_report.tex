\section*{Abstract}
\addcontentsline{toc}{section}{Abstract}
\vspace{0.2in}

\begin{spacing}{1.4}
\large
This project report describes the implementation of Sentinel Qubits in Quantum Key Distribution as a countermeasure against adaptive eavesdropping. Current security architectures rely on rigid 11\% Quantum Bit Error Rate (QBER) safety thresholds, which are proven to be vulnerable to selective attacking strategies that hide within the natural noise floor of long-haul fiber optic links. To mitigate this vulnerability, our research introduces a "Sentinel Stream"—a curated set of non-cryptographic qubits that serve as a real-time environmental noise baseline. By computing the statistical "Error Fingerprint" between data and sentinel qubits, we decouple natural decoherence from adversarial interaction. We deliver a rigorous mathematical framework leveraging Hoeffding bounds to validate detection confidence in practical finite-key regimes.
\end{spacing}

\section{Introduction}

\subsection{The Threshold Paradox}
The primary limitation of contemporary QKD deployment is the "Threshold Paradox." In any practical fiber system, accidental detectors counts and fiber decoherence introduce significant levels of noise. Because standard protocol logic cannot distinguish between natural decoherence and malicious measurement, the entire system must terminate if the error rate crosses a fixed threshold. This results in high key abortion rates, especially in noisy urban fibers where polarization fluctuations often approach the 11\% limit.

\subsection{Project Scope (QT002)}
QT002 introduces the "Sentinel Strategy." By interspersing publicly verifiable "sentinel qubits" into the qubit stream, we establish a baseline for the channel's current noise characteristics. If the error rate on the secret key qubits exceeds that of the sentinels by a statistically significant margin, we can infer the presence of an eavesdropper, regardless of whether the total QBER is still within "safe" limits.

\section{Sentinel Strategy Architecture}

\subsection{Protocol Flow with Sentinels}
The sentinel mask is a pseudo-random bitstream generated by Alice and Bob during the sifting phase.
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Index Mapping}: Alice chooses a fraction $p_s$ (set to 20\%) of the transmitted slots as "Sentinels".
    \item \textbf{Basis Agreement}: After transmission, Alice and Bob perform basis sifting as per standard BB84.
    \item \textbf{Sentinel Disclosure}: Alice reveals the indices and intended states of the sentinel slots.
    \item \textbf{Noise Estimation}: Bob calculates $QBER_{sentinel}$ exclusively from these known states.
    \item \textbf{Cross-Correlation}: The remaining bits (keys) are treated as unknown, and $QBER_{key}$ is calculated for these separately.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Information Asymmetry}
The core strength of the strategy is that Eve is unaware of which qubits are sentinels during her measurement interaction. Any interaction she makes with the stream will affect sentinels and data qubits equally. This allows the sentinel stream to act as a "Calibration Baseline" for all other security decisions.

\section{Mathematical Methodology}

\subsection{The Delta-Fingerprint}
We define the Error Delta $\Delta E$ as the absolute difference between the two monitored streams. The mathematical fingerprint $\vec{f}$ is defined as a vector of three temporal features:
\begin{equation}
    \Delta E = |E_{key} - E_{sentinel}|
\end{equation}
Under pure stochastic noise, $\mathbb{E}[\Delta E] \approx 0$. If an adversary targets only the secret key portion (an impossible feat without knowing the mask), or if her measurement statistics differ from the thermal decoherence profile, $\Delta E$ will deviate significantly.

\subsection{Hoeffding Bounds for Confidence}
In practical systems, we must make decisions on finite block sizes (e.g., 1000 qubits). We apply the Hoeffding bound to establish the probability that an observed delta is a statistical anomaly:
\begin{equation}
    \text{Pr}(|\Delta E| \ge \epsilon) \le 2\exp(-2N\epsilon^2)
\end{equation}
Where $N$ is the block size and $\epsilon$ is the detection threshold. This allows us to set a statistical "Confidence Floor" (typically $1-\alpha$, where $\alpha=0.05$). Any delta exceeding this bound is flagged as a likely adversarial signature.

\section{Results and Analysis}

\subsection{Detection Sensitivity in Noisy Channels}
We executed simulations across a wide range of channel noise environments ($p_{depolarize} \in [0.01, 0.15]$). Our results demonstrate that as the noise floor rises, the Hoeffding confidence bound naturally expands, preventing "False Positives" while maintaining sensitivity to intrusions.

\begin{figure}[h]

\caption{Cross-Correlation Fingerprinting. Benign noise trials (gray) cluster within the Hoeffding safety bound, while sub-threshold attacks (colored) are clearly separated.}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Efficiency Trade-off}
A critical finding of QT002 is the relationship between sentinel density and detection confidence. We analyzed the sifting efficiency $R$ relative to sentinel probability $p_s$:
\begin{equation}
    R = 0.5 \cdot (1 - p_s)
\end{equation}
While high $p_s$ improves detection accuracy, it reduces raw key rate. We found that $p_s = 20\%$ provides the highest "Security Efficiency," where the gain in detected sub-threshold intrusions outweighs the loss in raw slot density.

\section{Conclusion}

QT002 establishes that temporal sentinel fingerprinting provides a more robust security metric than simple scalar QBER thresholds. By decoupling natural channel decoherence from malicious interaction through statistical benchmarks, we have established a path toward more resilient quantum communication networks. The data generated by this monitoring suite is directly consumed by the AECE classifier developed in QT003.
