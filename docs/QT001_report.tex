\section*{Abstract}
\addcontentsline{toc}{section}{Abstract}
\vspace{0.2in}

\begin{spacing}{1.2}
This project report abstracts the foundational research for the BB84 Quantum Key Distribution (QKD) protocol, focusing on the identification and simulation of sophisticated sub-threshold threat models. While theoretical BB84 offers unconditional information-theoretic security, practical fiber-optic links introduce a significant "Security Gap" due to natural decoherence and standardized error-abort thresholds (typically 11\%). We develop and present a high-performance vectorized simulation engine that models the polarization drift and stochastic depolarization characteristic of long-haul fiber links. Within this framework, we analyze the performance of an adaptive eavesdropper capable of concealing information-extraction activities within the channel's natural noise floor. By extracting high-fidelity statistical snapshots of the protocol's sifting efficiency and Quantum Bit Error Rate (QBER), this document establishes the mathematical and simulation baseline required for the subsequent implementation of intelligent adversarial detection engines.
\end{spacing}

\section{Introduction}

\subsection{Quantum Key Distribution Overview}
The security of modern digital infrastructure traditionally rests on mathematical complexity. However, the advent of quantum computation threatens many of these classical cryptographic standards. Quantum Key Distribution (QKD) provides a counter-measure by deriving security not from computational difficulty, but from the physical properties of quantum systems. The core goal is to enable two parties—Alice and Bob—to generate a shared secret key with the guarantee that any eavesdropper, Eve, will be detected if she attempts to intercept the key material.

\subsection{The 11\% Threshold Limitation}
The most prominent challenge in deploying QKD over practical fiber networks is the management of natural channel noise. In standardized protocols like BB84, any Quantum Bit Error Rate (QBER) below 11\% is typically treated as benign decoherence, while rates above this threshold trigger a system abort. This static binary logic creates a vulnerable regime where an adversary can selectively measure a small percentage of qubits, keeping the total QBER just below 11\%, and extracting meaningful info-theoretic entropy without being flagged.

\subsection{Project Scope (QT001)}
QT001 focuses on the core protocol simulation. The objectives are:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Implementation of a high-speed vectorized state math engine in Python.
    \item Modeling of Markovian noise processes in long-haul fiber links.
    \item Quantitative analysis of the "Security Gap" through various adaptive threat models.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Protocol Core Design}

\subsection{Vectorized Simulation Framework}
Traditional qubit-by-qubit simulations are computationally inefficient for large-scale Monte Carlo analysis. We utilize a vectorized approach where the entire session (up to $10^6$ qubits) is represented as a tensor.
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{States}: Represented by 2D rotation matrices $R(\theta)$.
    \item \textbf{Decoherence}: Modeled as stochastic rotations acting on the entire session vector.
    \item \textbf{Sifting}: Performed via boolean indexing, allowing for near-instantaneous post-processing of massive frames.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Polarization Drift Modeling}
Fiber-optic channels exhibit random polarization transformations. We model the channel operator $\mathcal{C}$ as a sequence of three rotations around the $x, y, z$ axes of the Bloch sphere:
\begin{equation}
    \rho_{out} = R_z(\alpha) R_y(\beta) R_x(\gamma) \rho_{in} R_x^T(\gamma) R_y^T(\beta) R_z^T(\alpha)
\end{equation}
In our simulation, $\alpha, \beta, \gamma$ are evolved over time using a Weiner process to simulate the slow drift and fast bursts characteristic of urban fiber environments.

\section{Threat Modeling: The Adaptive Eve}

\subsection{The Intercept-Resend (IR) Baseline}
In the simplest attack, Eve measures every photon in a randomly chosen basis. If her basis matches Alice's, she gains the bit; if not, she introduces a new qubit to Bob. This introduces a 25\% QBER, which is easily detected by Bob during the sifting phase.

\subsection{Sub-Threshold Intelligence}
The more dangerous threat vector is what we term the "Selective Interaction" attack. Eve targets only a fraction $q$ of the qubits. Her objective function is:
\begin{equation}
    \text{Maximize } I(A:E) \quad \text{subject to } QBER(A:B) < 11\%
\end{equation}
Where $QBER_{total} = QBER_{channel} + q \cdot 0.25$. If the channel is high quality ($QBER_{channel} \approx 3\%$), Eve can interact with up to $32\%$ of the qubits while staying below the 11\% threshold.

\subsection{Markovian Attack Bursts}
Sophisticated adversaries do not attack uniformly. By clustering measurements into temporal "bursts," they attempt to mimic the bursty nature of detector dark counts or thermal fiber events. This spectral similarity makes simple thresholding even less effective.

\section{Results and Analysis}

\subsection{Sifting Efficiency Patterns}
We analyzed the sifting efficiency across different noise regimes. As expected, the ratio of sifted bits to raw bits centers around 0.5. However, under high-noise regimes ($>8\%$), the statistical variance in block-wise QBER increases, creating Windows of Opportunity for sub-threshold attacks.

\begin{figure}[h]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.85\linewidth]{fingerprint_analysis.png}
\caption{Visualization of Eavesdropping Windows. Even when mean QBER is safe (blue), individual block fluctuations (colored markers) can hide significant adversarial interaction.}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Information Leakage Quantization}
The information Eve extracts is non-linear relative to her interaction density. We quantify this using the mutual information $I(A:E)$:
\begin{equation}
    I(A:E) = q \cdot (1 - h(QBER_{measure}))
\end{equation}
Where $h(\cdot)$ is the binary entropy function. Our results demonstrate that as little as a 4\% increase in total QBER (from 3\% to 7\%) allows Eve to extract enough entropy to potentially break short-key classical encryption if privacy amplification is not adjusted dynamically.
